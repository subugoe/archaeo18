Gladiator, dying, is a most valuable monument of ancient sculpture. This man, when he had
received t mortal stroke, is particularly careful, ut procumbat honeste, that he might fall
gracefully; he is seated in a reclining posture on the ground, & has just strength sufficient
to support himself on his right arm; & in his expiring moments, it is plainly seen that he
does not abandon himself to grief & dejection, but is solicitous to maintain that firmness
of aspect, which the gladiators valued themselves on preserving in this season of distress, &
that attitude wh. they had learned of the masters o defence. He fears not death, nor seems to
betray any tokens o fear by his countentance, nor to shed one tear: "quis mediocris gladiator
ingemuit, quis vultum mutavit usquam, quis non modo stitit, verum etiam decubuit
turpiter", says Cicero, in that part of his Tusculan, where he is describing t astonishing firm-
ness o these persons. We see, in this instance, notwithstanding his remaining strength, that
he has but a moment to live, & we view him with attention, that we may see him expire
& fall: thus the ancients knew how to animate marble & to give it almost every ex-
pression of life. Rus's Cyclop.-

Niobe. Ultima restabat, quam toto corpore mater,
Tota veste tegens, unam, minimamque relinque;
De multis minimam posco, clamavit, et unam! Ovid.
The Statue and the verses form an excellent commentary upon each other.
"The verse and sculpture bore an equal part,
And art reflected images to art." Matthews's Diary. 1. p. 50.
